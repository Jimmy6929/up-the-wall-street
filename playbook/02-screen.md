# 02 — Screen: Green Flags vs. Red Flags (ch. 08–09)

The perfect stock is usually **boring, ugly, ignored, or unpleasant** — because neglect keeps it cheap until earnings force attention. The dangerous stock is **exciting, glamorous, crowded, and widely-touted** — because the hope is already in the price. Screen every candidate for both, and weigh the **emotional tell**: feeling thrilled is a yellow flag; feeling bored is often green.

## Green flags — the 13 attributes of a perfect stock (ch. 08)

1. It **sounds dull** — or even ridiculous (a boring/silly name keeps the crowd away).
2. It **does something dull.**
3. It **does something disagreeable** (waste, grime, guts) — repels competitors and analysts.
4. It's a **spinoff** (neglected, mispriced, set up to succeed).
5. **Institutions don't own it and analysts don't follow it** — no crowding, room to rise.
6. **Rumors abound** (toxic waste, the mafia) — off-putting associations keep it cheap.
7. There's **something depressing** about it (e.g., funeral services).
8. It's a **no-growth industry** (flat industries deter competition; Lynch *prefers* a moderate grower in a no-growth field to a hot grower in a hot field).
9. **It's got a niche** — a defensible moat (local monopoly, unique franchise) that protects pricing.
10. **People have to keep buying it** — repeat-purchase staples (drugs, razors, soft drinks) = durable recurring revenue.
11. **It's a user of technology** (not a maker) — benefits from falling tech costs without the brutal competition makers face.
12. **Insiders are buying** — the people who know best, putting their own money in.
13. **The company is buying back its own shares** — shrinks share count, lifts per-share earnings, signals management thinks it's cheap.

## Red flags — stocks to avoid (ch. 09)

- **The hottest stock in the hottest industry.** Avoid above all. Glamour bids the price past support; hot industries draw competition that destroys the margins everyone is chasing; these fall hardest when momentum breaks.
- **"The next [famous winner]"** ("the next IBM / McDonald's / Nvidia"). Almost never is — and the label often marks the peak of both companies.
- **Diworseifications** — a profitable company blowing cash on overpriced, unrelated acquisitions. (Silver lining: the *victims* can later become turnarounds or asset plays.)
- **Whisper stocks / long shots** — a thrilling, world-changing story (a cure, a revolution) with little or no earnings behind it. The story is the bait.
- **Single-customer dependence** — most revenue from one big customer is one lost contract from disaster. Revenue concentration is risk concentration.

## How to run the screen

1. Tally which **green flags** are present (niche, repeat-purchase, insider buying, buybacks, low institutional ownership, spinoff, tech-user are the highest-signal ones).
2. Tally which **red flags** are present. **Any** of: hottest-in-hottest, "the next X", whisper/no-earnings, heavy insider selling + 90%+ institutional ownership → this strongly points to **Avoid**, regardless of a superficially okay PEG.
3. **Excitement check:** if the case rests on how exciting the story is, downgrade and re-examine the earnings.

## Output of this step
- **Green flags found** (list).
- **Red flags found** (list).
- **Excitement check** result (is the thesis leaning on thrill?).
- A provisional lean: *promising / mixed / avoid* — to be confirmed by the numbers and the checklist.
