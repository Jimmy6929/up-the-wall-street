# BR — Broadridge Financial Solutions, Inc.

> [!summary] Verdict: **Watchlist**
> The highest-quality of the three scan finalists — a genuine **wide-moat near-monopoly** (it processes the vast majority of US shareholder proxies), with a **sound balance sheet** (1.9× leverage, below target, investment-grade), a growing dividend, and a moat arguably **extending into digital**. But it is **not cheap**: PEG **2.06** on GAAP (~1.6 on adjusted EPS) for ~10% EPS growth that is really **~5–6% organic** plus buybacks plus a fading event-driven tailwind — with **closed sales (bookings) cut ~15–20%** and **90% crowded ownership**. A great business at a rich price and a decelerating moment → the textbook **Watchlist** case. Buy only on a meaningful de-rate (PEG toward ~1) and/or re-accelerating bookings.

## 1. Lead (ch. 06)
Surfaced by the `/scan` (PEG 0.54, ranked #42; the critic flagged it as a dull near-monopoly with sticky recurring revenue). **A lead is not a buy signal** — and the homework shows the screen's cheap PEG was an artifact (a bogus 34% CAGR built on a single-quarter base year).

## 2. Classification (ch. 07)
**Category:** **Stalwart.** Broadridge is the back-office utility of US capital markets — dominant in investor communications (proxy processing / regulatory & shareholder comms) plus trade-processing and wealth technology — a large-cap (~$17.5B) regulated near-monopoly spun out of ADP (2007). Recurring revenue grows high-single-digits and adjusted EPS ~10%, with a long dividend-growth record. That is the textbook stalwart (steady ~10–12%, high quality, downside protection) — **not** the "Fast grower" the scan guessed (its 34% CAGR was a base-year error; real GAAP CAGR is 11.2%). **Expectation set:** stalwart → steady gains, *not* a tenbagger; **the key issue is PRICE** (is the PEG reasonable?). **Effect on whole company:** it's big, so big moves are unlikely; growth comes from new-tech sales + regulatory/governance volume + tuck-in M&A.

## 3. Screen (ch. 08–09)
- **Green flags:** **wide-moat near-monopoly niche** (#9 — >90% of US proxy distribution); "does something dull" (#2 — financial back-office plumbing); **sticky, recurring, multi-year contracted revenue** (#10-like); **user of technology** (#11); long **buyback + dividend-growth** record (#13, dividend $3.90/yr and rising, ~2.6% yield); boring/under-followed by retail; moat **extending into digital** (pass-through voting, tokenized-securities proxy run on Broadridge's rails).
- **Red flags:** **rich price** (PEG ~2.0 GAAP / ~1.6 adjusted) — paying up for quality; **90% institutional ownership** (crowded — fully discovered, the opposite of a Lynch setup); **organic deceleration** (closed sales −19% YoY, FY26 bookings guidance cut ~15–20%; organic recurring ~5–6%); EPS growth **flattered by buybacks + a cyclical event-driven spike** that management expects to normalize; tuck-in **M&A dependence** (mild diworseification risk).
- **Excitement check:** **low — green.** Boring financial infrastructure; the thesis rests on durable recurring earnings, not thrill. The risk here is **paying up for quality**, not hype.

## 5. The two-minute drill (ch. 11)
1. **What it is:** Broadridge is the back-office utility of Wall Street — it processes the large majority of US shareholder proxy votes and investor communications, and runs trade-processing and wealth-management technology for banks and brokers.
2. **Why it grows:** sticky, recurring, multi-year contracted revenue (regulatory-driven proxy/comms volume) compounding ~5–8% organically, plus tuck-in acquisitions and a steady buyback + dividend-growth record → ~10% adjusted EPS growth. The moat is extending into digital (pass-through voting, tokenized-securities proxy) — which runs *through* Broadridge's platform, reinforcing the moat rather than threatening it.
3. **What breaks it:** **paying too high a price** (PEG ~2) for an organic engine that is **decelerating** (bookings cut, ~5–6% organic, a cyclical event-driven tailwind normalizing); a regulatory cut to the NYSE-set proxy distribution fees (slow-moving but real); over-reliance on M&A; and multiple de-rating if growth slows.

**Headline story (watchlist):** *"The back-office monopoly of Wall Street — wide moat, sound balance sheet, growing dividend, but richly priced (PEG ~2) for a decelerating organic engine. A quality stalwart to own when fear marks it down further."*

## 6. Bear case (the skeptic, ch. 09/20)
1. **You're paying a near-fast-grower multiple for a decelerating stalwart.** Q3 FY26 closed sales fell **19% YoY** ($58M vs $71M) and management **cut** FY26 closed-sales guidance ~15–20% (to $240–290M) in the same quarter it *raised* EPS guidance — the EPS raise is carried by buybacks and a cyclical event-driven spike, not bookings.
2. **Organic growth is mid-single-digit, not double-digit.** Q3 recurring revenue +6% cc (only ~1pt from acquisitions); the long-term *organic* recurring target is just 5–8%. The double-digit figure is EPS, levered by buybacks. Capital Markets (within GTO) grew ~0%.
3. **A fading tailwind is flattering the numbers.** Event-driven revenue hit $73M in Q3 on "elevated" activity; management guides normalization to ~$60M/quarter. This volatile, cyclical line has swung ±27% YoY.
4. **The regulated-fee moat is politically contentious.** Proxy distribution fees are set under NYSE Rules 451/465 and SEC-reviewed; Broadridge's >90% share has been "accused of monopolistic pricing." Reform risk is real but historically modest/slow (the 2013 reform was a ~4% blended cut).
5. **Fully discovered.** 90% institutional ownership and a bullish sell-side — no "undiscovered" angle; the quality is fully recognized and priced in.

**Bull points the skeptic could NOT kill (intellectual honesty):** the **balance sheet is not strained** (1.9× leverage, below target, IG, strong FCF — the per-share net-debt figure overstates risk); and the **moat-disruption thesis is weak** — pass-through voting and tokenized-securities proxy both run *through* Broadridge's infrastructure, extending the moat.

**The single thing the note must not get wrong:** do **not** credit the "10–12% EPS growth" as durable *organic* growth — it's ~5–6% organic plus buybacks plus an above-normal event-driven spike, in a quarter where forward bookings were cut. Price the *organic* rate against the rich PEG. (The skeptic landed on Watchlist, not Pass — nothing is structurally broken.)

## 8. Verdict & sell triggers
**Verdict: Watchlist** — justified on fundamentals: a genuinely **high-quality, wide-moat near-monopoly** with a **sound balance sheet** and a growing dividend (the best business of the three scan finalists), but **richly priced** (PEG 2.06 GAAP / ~1.6 adjusted) for **~10% EPS growth that is only ~5–6% organic**, with **bookings cut ~15–20%**, a **fading event-driven tailwind**, and **90% crowded ownership**. Good business, wrong price (and a decelerating moment) — the textbook Watchlist case. The scan's apparent cheapness was a base-year artifact. This is the one to **own when fear marks it down to a fair price** (ch.17), not chase here.

**Move to Buy candidate if** (ideally several):
- **Price/multiple de-rates so PEG approaches ~1** on adjusted EPS (roughly a ~14× adjusted multiple — a meaningfully lower entry for the same ~10% growth);
- **Closed sales (bookings) re-accelerate** for ~2 quarters and the >$1B pipeline converts to recurring revenue;
- **Organic recurring-revenue growth re-accelerates** toward the high end of the 5–8% range (not masked by event-driven spikes);
- The growing dividend stays well-covered by FCF.

**Drop to Pass if** organic recurring growth stalls toward low-single-digits, leverage climbs above the 2.5× target to fund buybacks/M&A, or a material proxy-fee reform cuts the regulated-fee economics. Until a trigger fires: watch, don't buy. *(Do not treat the $139.79 52-week low as a floor — the verdict is PEG-based, not price-based.)*
